# Context for creating story and cards

## Card Guidelines

A card is a single swipeable screen within a story. It is the atomic unit of the learning experience — one idea, one moment, one step forward in the narrative.

---

### Card Anatomy

Every card has up to four elements:

| Element      | Role                                              | Required? |
| ------------ | ------------------------------------------------- | --------- |
| **Visual**   | Dominant image or graphic - carries the mood      | Yes       |
| **Headline** | Core message in one line - what the student keeps | Yes       |
| **Body**     | 1-3 sentences of supporting context               | Optional  |
| **Prompt**   | A question or micro-action inviting engagement    | Optional  |

If a card needs more than these four elements, it is carrying too much — split it.

---

### Card Types

Different moments in a story call for different card types:

#### Hook Card

Opens the story. Creates immediate curiosity or recognition.

- Lead with a striking visual or a bold question
- Headline is provocative, not informative
- Body is optional — sometimes the image and headline are enough
- Example headline: *"You're about to make a decision that could cost €200k. What do you actually know?"*

#### Situation Card

Establishes the scenario, character, or context the student will follow.

- Introduce who, where, and what is at stake
- Keep it concrete — a name, a role, a specific moment
- Visual should ground the setting

#### Tension Card

Introduces the problem, conflict, or gap in knowledge.

- This is where the student starts to feel the question
- Avoid giving the answer — just make the tension clear
- Prompt is effective here: *"What would you do?"* / *"What's missing here?"*

#### Concept Card

Introduces a key idea — briefly, through the lens of the story.

- Name the concept, but anchor it in the scenario
- One concept per card, maximum
- Body can include a short definition or contrast
- Never list multiple concepts on a single card

#### Data Card

Presents a statistic, finding, or fact that adds weight to the story.

- Let the number be the headline — make it land visually
- Body provides brief context: source, timeframe, what it means
- Keep it to one data point per card

#### Reflection Card

Invites the student to pause and connect.

- Used near the end of a story
- A single open question with no right answer
- No body text — the question should stand alone
- Example: *"When did you last see this in real life?"*

#### Cliffhanger Card

The final card. Closes the story without resolving it.

- Ends on the central lesson question
- Creates the feeling of an open loop the student will want to close in class
- Visual should feel forward-looking or unresolved
- Example: *"So — is Porter's Five Forces still useful in a world of platforms? We'll find out."*

---

### Writing Principles

**Headlines are everything.**
A student who only reads the headline should still take something away. Write the headline first. If it doesn't work on its own, rewrite it before touching the body.

**One idea per card.**
Not one topic — one idea. The difference: a topic is "pricing strategy"; an idea is "price anchoring makes €99 feel cheaper than €100, even when we know the trick." Lead with the idea, not the category.

**Body text is a privilege, not a default.**
Add body text only when the headline cannot carry the meaning alone. When in doubt, cut it. Short body = 1–3 sentences max. No paragraphs.

**Prompts invite, not test.**
A prompt on a card is not a quiz question. It should feel like a conversation opener: *"Sound familiar?"*, *"What would you change?"*, *"Have you seen this?"* Low stakes, high invitation.

**Write for a 10-second read.**
If reading the card takes longer than 10–15 seconds, it has too much text. Cut until it doesn't.

**Every card needs emotional charge.**
A student who feels nothing will choose nothing. The three student decisions only become meaningful when the card creates a feeling — tension, surprise, recognition, or discomfort. Without that charge, the student has nothing to act on.

- **Tension** keeps something unresolved. The student feels they need to come back to it.
- **Surprise or recognition** makes something click. The student feels it's worth keeping.
- **Wonder or an open pull** draws the student forward. They want to know how this ends.

Before finalising a card, ask: *what does the student feel when they finish reading this?* If the answer is "nothing in particular" — the card is not ready.

**Headlines provoke; bodies deepen.**
The headline should create or land the emotional beat. The body, when present, should deepen that feeling — not just add information. A body that explains without resonating is dead weight.

---

### Visual Direction

Each card should be conceived with a visual in mind. When writing a card, note what the visual should convey:

- **Mood:** tense, curious, familiar, surprising
- **Subject:** person, place, object, data visualisation, abstract graphic
- **Composition:** simple and bold — one focal point
- Avoid stock-photo clichés (handshakes, lightbulbs, generic offices)
- Prefer: real-world settings, relatable characters, clean data visuals

---

### Tone per Card Type

| Card type    | Tone                           |
| ------------ | ------------------------------ |
| Hook         | Bold, provocative, urgent      |
| Situation    | Grounded, clear, scene-setting |
| Tension      | Unsettled, questioning, open   |
| Concept      | Precise, confident, anchored   |
| Data         | Surprising, factual, visual    |
| Reflection   | Calm, open, personal           |
| Cliffhanger  | Forward-looking, unresolved    |

---

### Student Actions

After reading each card, the student makes a self-assessment:

| Action                        | What it means                                     | Effect on cheatsheet                              |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| **I already know this**       | "I'm familiar with this — I've got it"            | Captured briefly; lower depth and priority        |
| **I really don't care**       | "I don't see why this matters to me"              | Captured; flagged — relevance not yet clear       |
| **I don't know**              | "This is new or unclear to me"                    | Captured with more depth and higher priority      |

Every card ends up on the cheatsheet. The student's action determines the order and depth — not whether the content is captured. This makes the action a form of metacognition: the student is assessing their own knowledge and engagement, not filtering what they keep.

#### Design implications

**"I already know this" is honest** when the headline is precise enough that the student can genuinely assess their familiarity — not just recognise a word, but actually understand the concept. Vague headlines make this action meaningless: a student can say "I already know this" about anything they've vaguely heard of.

**"I really don't care" is hard to choose** when the card makes relevance personal and immediate. If a student reaches for this action, the card has failed to connect. A card that feels abstract, distant from their work, or obviously irrelevant invites this response. That is a design problem, not a student problem.

**"I don't know" is genuine** when the card introduces something counter-intuitive, precise, or new — something the student could not have safely assumed. A card where every student picks "I already know this" is either too familiar or too vague. It is teaching nothing.

Emotional charge is what makes the self-assessment honest. A card that creates surprise, tension, or recognition pushes the student to respond truthfully — not to pick the most comfortable option.

---

### Quality Checklist

Before finalising a card:

- [ ] Headline works on its own — a student could screenshot just this and still get the idea
- [ ] One idea only — no concept stacking
- [ ] Body is 3 sentences or fewer (or absent)
- [ ] Visual direction is specified
- [ ] Headline is precise enough that "I already know this" requires real familiarity — not just recognition of a word
- [ ] Card makes its relevance personal enough that "I really don't care" is genuinely hard to choose
- [ ] Card introduces something specific enough that "I don't know" is a real and honest option
- [ ] Fits its role in the story arc (hook / build / cliffhanger)
- [ ] Readable in 10–15 seconds
- [ ] Tone matches the moment in the story

## Educational principles

- Relevant — grounded in current topics and real-world contexts
- Challenging — pushes students to think critically and go beyond the basics
- Insightful — fosters deep understanding rather than surface-level knowledge
- Transfer-oriented — helps students apply what they learn to new situations
- Engaging — captures attention and stimulates curiosity
- Visual — uses graphics and visuals to support comprehension

## Student Personas — Business Bachelor, HZ University of Applied Sciences

These two archetypes represent the range of students typically found in the business bachelor programmes at HZ (e.g., Business Administration, International Business, Commerce). Use them to calibrate lesson difficulty, exercise design, and instructional tone.

---

### Persona 1 — Sanne (HAVO instroom)

**Age:** 19
**Background:** Straight from HAVO. Chose a business programme as a broad, pragmatic step toward a career in management, marketing, or consulting. No substantial professional work experience yet beyond a student job.

**Strengths:**

- Comfortable with structured assignments and clear grading criteria
- Can handle theory reasonably well when it is well-framed and connects to a career outcome
- Strong in written tasks, presentations, and group coordination when the task is well-defined
- Motivated by grades and future career prospects

**Challenges:**

- Limited real business experience — abstract models (e.g., Porter, BCG matrix) feel disconnected from reality
- Struggles with ambiguous or ill-structured tasks where there is no single right answer
- Tends to reproduce theory rather than critically apply or challenge it
- Risk of surface learning: memorising frameworks without understanding their conditions or limitations
- Low tolerance for topics that don't feel immediately "useful"

**Motivation:** Extrinsically driven — responds to grades, career relevance, and peer comparison. Intrinsic motivation activates when the subject connects clearly to a role she can imagine herself in.

**Design implication:** Needs worked examples before open tasks. Make the career relevance of each concept explicit. Use case studies with recognisable contexts (retail, services, SME). Reward analysis, not only description.

---

### Persona 2 — Elias (MBO instroom / work experience)

**Age:** 24
**Background:** Completed MBO-4 in a commercial direction (e.g., retail management, logistics, or business services) and has 2–3 years of work experience in a customer-facing or operational role before entering HBO. May also represent an international student from Belgium, Germany, or further afield.

**Strengths:**

- Strong practical intuition — has navigated real business situations, handled customers, managed priorities
- High inner drive; returned to education with a deliberate goal (career advancement, deeper knowledge)
- Brings credible examples from his own work history into group discussions
- Comfortable with ambiguity and "good enough" decisions under time pressure
- Mature perspective in group work and professional communication

**Challenges:**

- Can underestimate the value of theory ("I already do this at work") — resists frameworks that feel abstract or bureaucratic
- May have gaps in analytical and academic skills: structuring arguments, referencing, quantitative reasoning
- Can become frustrated in lessons that move slowly or repeat things he already knows from practice
- Group dynamics: sometimes dominates discussions with experience, or disengages when the task feels too school-like
- If international: additional language and cultural navigation on top of academic demands

**Motivation:** Wants to grow from practitioner to strategist — from doing to deciding. Responds well when his experience is treated as a legitimate knowledge source, not a deficit to be corrected.

**Design implication:** Anchor theory in real business situations first. Acknowledge prior experience as data. Use tasks that require moving from description to analysis to recommendation. Give him room to lead on applied components.

### Neurodiversity & Universal Design for Learning (UDL)

In HBO business programmes, a meaningful share of students is neurodivergent — including students with ADHD, dyslexia, anxiety, or concentration difficulties. Neurodivergent students are present across both archetypes; they are not a separate group but a dimension within every classroom.

The guidance below is based on the ECIO framework *Handreiking Neuro-inclusief Onderwijs* (2024) and the Universal Design for Learning (UDL) principles it applies.

#### The UDL Mindset

UDL shifts the question from *"how do we accommodate this student?"* to *"how do we design so barriers don't arise in the first place?"* What helps neurodivergent students — predictability, multiple formats, explicit structure, choice — improves learning for everyone.

UDL works through three lenses:

**1. Multiple Means of Engagement (the "Why")**
Students engage when they understand why something matters, feel psychologically safe, and have some control over their learning path.

- Make learning objectives and relevance explicit at the start
- Offer choice where possible (project theme, output format, group vs. individual)
- Establish predictable lesson rhythms and clear assessment criteria
- Build in self-reflection moments so students can locate themselves

**2. Multiple Means of Representation (the "What")**
Students process information differently. Presenting concepts in only one modality creates unnecessary barriers.

- Pair verbal explanation with visuals, diagrams, or business models/frameworks
- Provide written summaries alongside spoken instruction
- Use recaps and prior-knowledge activators at the start of each lesson
- Avoid walls of text — chunk content into small, labelled sections

**3. Multiple Means of Action & Expression (the "How")**
Students demonstrate competence differently. Restricting output to one format systematically disadvantages some students.

- Allow varied submission formats: report, presentation, advice memo, poster, oral explanation
- Provide templates, rubrics, and intermediate feedback moments
- Support both collaborative and individual contribution in group tasks
- Give structured choices rather than fully open briefs (reduces cognitive load without removing autonomy)

#### Neurodiversity Signals per Persona

| Signal to watch for       | Sanne (HAVO)                         | Elias (MBO / work experience)         |
| ------------------------- | ------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------- |
| Cognitive overload        | High — too much new at once          | Lower, spikes on abstract theory      |
| Need for structure        | High — needs visible lesson flow     | Moderate — purpose-driven only        |
| Sensory/attention needs   | Often undetected (compliant)         | May mask from work experience         |
| Output format flexibility | Defaults to what is "expected"       | Prefers practical over formal essays  |

#### Practical Design Checklist (apply to every lesson)

- [ ] Learning objectives stated explicitly at the start
- [ ] Lesson structure visible (e.g., agenda on first slide)
- [ ] Core concept presented in at least two formats (visual + verbal or model + example)
- [ ] At least one moment of low-stakes self-assessment (quiz, exit ticket, reflection question)
- [ ] Assignment instructions available in writing, not only spoken
- [ ] At least one element of student choice (topic, format, depth, or pace)
- [ ] Assessment criteria made explicit before the task, not only after

## Story Guidelines

A story is a swipeable sequence of cards — like an Instagram or LinkedIn story — that students move through to prepare for an upcoming lesson. The story is the container; cards are its building blocks.

---

### Purpose

A story gives students a low-effort, high-impact way to activate prior knowledge, encounter the core question of the lesson, and arrive in class curious rather than blank. It is not a summary of the lesson — it is a spark before it.

---

### Narrative Arc

Every story follows a three-act structure:

**Act 1 — Hook (1–2 cards)**
Open with something surprising, provocative, or recognisable. A striking statistic, a dilemma, a question that has no obvious answer. The goal is to make the student feel: *this is worth paying attention to.*

**Act 2 — Build (3–6 cards)**
Introduce the key tension or concept through a short narrative. Use a character, scenario, or situation the student can follow. Each card advances the story one step — it does not dump information. The student should feel momentum: *I want to know what happens next.*

**Act 3 — Cliffhanger or Invitation (1 card)**
End without resolving everything. Pose the central question the lesson will explore. Make the student feel the gap in their knowledge — and want to close it. This is the bridge into class.

---

### Story Design Principles

**One thread, one tension.**
A story has a single narrative line. It does not cover all lesson content — it sets up one central question or problem. Resist the urge to be comprehensive.

**Student as the protagonist.**
Frame the story from the perspective of someone the student can identify with — a young professional, a startup founder, a manager making a call. Sanne should see a future version of herself; Elias should recognise a situation from his own work experience.

**Show, don't tell.**
Use concrete situations over abstract definitions. A character facing a real decision is more powerful than a bullet list of theory. Introduce concepts through the situation, not before it.

**Progressive disclosure.**
Each card reveals one new piece of information or raises one new question. Do not front-load. Let the story breathe.

**Low cognitive load per card.**
Students are swiping, not studying. Each card should be digestible in 10–15 seconds. Save depth for the lesson itself.

**Visual-first.**
Every card should work with a strong visual. Write as if the image carries half the meaning. Text is a caption, not a lecture.

---

### Story Length

| Story type        | Cards  | Estimated swipe time |
| ----------------- | ------ | -------------------- |
| Short intro       | 4–6    | ~1 minute            |
| Standard pre-read | 7–10   | 2–3 minutes          |
| Deep dive         | 11–15  | 3–5 minutes          |

Default to the standard range. A story that takes more than 5 minutes to swipe through will lose students.

---

### Tone

- Conversational, not academic
- Confident, not preachy
- Curious, not conclusive
- Direct — no filler sentences

Write as if you are telling a colleague something interesting you just discovered, not writing a textbook.

---

### Alignment with Educational Principles

| Story element    | Educational principle                        |
| ---------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| Hook card        | Engaging — captures attention                |
| Narrative arc    | Relevant — grounded in real-world context    |
| Build cards      | Insightful — fosters deeper understanding    |
| Cliffhanger      | Transfer-oriented — sets up active learning  |
| Overall design   | Challenging — prompts critical thinking      |
